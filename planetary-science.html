Learn more about Planetary Science!

Terrestrial Planets

Terrestrial planets, including Mercury, Venus, Earth, and Mars, are rocky worlds with solid surfaces. This subdivision examines their geology, atmospheres, and potential for life. Studies of these planets provide insights into planetary formation, evolution, and the conditions necessary for habitability.

Gas Giants

Gas giants, such as Jupiter and Saturn, are massive planets composed primarily of hydrogen and helium. This field explores their complex atmospheres, magnetic fields, and extensive moon systems. Understanding gas giants helps scientists learn about the dynamics of giant planets and their role in the solar system.

Ice Giants

Ice giants, including Uranus and Neptune, are planets with cores of rock and ice surrounded by thick atmospheres. This subdivision delves into their unique compositions, extreme weather patterns, and intriguing magnetic fields. Research on ice giants expands our understanding of the diversity of planetary systems.

Dwarf Planets

Dwarf planets, like Pluto and Eris, are small celestial bodies that orbit the sun but have not cleared their orbital paths of other debris. This area of study focuses on their characteristics, such as their compositions, surfaces, and potential atmospheres, shedding light on the outer reaches of our solar system.

Planetary Moons

Planetary moons, or natural satellites, vary greatly in size and composition. This subdivision examines the diverse moons of the solar system, from Earth's Moon to the icy moons of Jupiter and Saturn. These studies provide critical insights into planetary processes and the potential for extraterrestrial life.

Planetary Rings

Planetary rings are composed of countless small particles orbiting around planets. This field investigates the formation, structure, and dynamics of ring systems, primarily around the gas giants. Research on planetary rings helps scientists understand the processes governing ring formation and evolution.
